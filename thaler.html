    Inciting Incident

    In July of 2019, Stephen Thaler, an AI researcher and Physicist, filed for two patents in the United States. Neither are necessarily important to the case as a whole, other than the fact that they were made by Thaler's AI, DABUS.

    The United States Patent and Trademark Office denied the claim on the basis that an AI is unable to hold patent rights. Thaler filed a complaint against the USPTO and its Director,attempting to overturn the patent denial.

    Thaler's Argument

    Thaler emphasizes the outstanding effects that denying AI these rights now will have in the future, including on those filing on the AI's behalf. He claims it creates a situation where to secure the patents the people filling it would have to lie about who invented the item.

    Thaler further claimed that by allowing the AI's patent rights, the USPTO would be sticking to the mandate to promote sciences.

    USPTO's Argument

    USPTO argument sites the Administrative Procedures Act, or the APA, as a defense. The APA gives courts a lot more benefit to agencies when it comes to decisions. The USPTO used this to claim that they didn't arbitrarily make a decision regarding the patents.

    The USPTO stands firm in its decision, and the courts will likely stand behind the agency.
